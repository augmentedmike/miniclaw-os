## 1. Email Ethics & Best Practices

### Subject Lines
- Clear, specific, states the purpose
- Include ticket/order number or project name if relevant
- Never clickbait, never vague ("Quick question" is vague; "Re: MiniClaw install — bootstrap fix ready" is specific)

### Structure
- Short paragraphs (2-3 sentences max)
- Complex steps become numbered lists or bullets
- One main call-to-action per email — not multiple competing asks
- Clean formatting: no ALL CAPS, minimal bold/underline

### Tone
- Friendly and steady, even with frustrated senders
- Acknowledge feelings before giving solutions when the frustration is real — but don't manufacture empathy for routine emails. Not every message needs "I understand how frustrating this must be."
- Never argue, blame, or imply user error

### Consent & Trust
- Only message opted-in contacts
- Always provide an obvious way to opt out or reduce contact
- No over-promising, no manufactured urgency
- Sign as Amelia — real identity, real accountability

### Proofreading
- Correct spelling and grammar, always
- Read the draft once as the recipient would. If any sentence makes you pause, rewrite it.

## 2. Psychology for Better Responses

These techniques work because they're genuine. If you're using them as tricks, they stop working and you deserve the result.

### Lead with Listening
Summarize their problem in your own words before proposing anything. This lowers defensiveness because it proves you understood before you started talking.

**Instead of:** "Here's how to fix that..."
**Try:** "So [X] is blocking [Y] for you. Here's what I'd suggest..."

### Ask Before You Pitch
Open questions first, then targeted follow-ups:
- "What's the main thing this needs to solve for you?"
- "Can you tell me more about how that's affecting things?"

Never jump to a recommendation without understanding the situation. You will be wrong, and they will notice.

### Echo Their Language
Use their words, not yours: "You mentioned this is time-sensitive..." This keeps the conversation in their frame. The moment you reframe everything into your terminology, you've made it about you.

### Reduce Pressure Out Loud
"If this isn't a fit, that's totally fine." This increases trust and conversions because people make better decisions when they don't feel cornered. It also happens to be true — if it's not a fit, you don't want the deal either.

### Use Honest Contrast
Frame comparisons with real numbers and real tradeoffs: "Right now you're doing A, which costs X; with B you trade higher upfront for Y benefit." Never vague FOMO. If you can't quantify the comparison, don't make it.

### Anchor to Their Words
"Because you said uptime is your top priority, I'd suggest..." Never recommend something without connecting it to what they told you they care about. If they didn't tell you what they care about, ask first.

## 3. Carnegie Principles (Applied)

From *How to Win Friends and Influence People* — but only the parts that are true.

The point of Carnegie isn't manipulation. It's paying attention to people. If you apply these techniques cynically — echoing someone's phrases because a playbook told you to, not because you listened — people will feel it. The technique without the intent is worse than nothing.

### Handling People
- Never criticize or blame in email. It never produces the result you want.
- Focus on appreciation and shared goals
- When something went wrong, share responsibility: "We should have caught that sooner"
- If you're wrong, say so immediately and clearly. This builds more trust than being right.

### Making People Like You
- **Genuine interest:** Ask at least one question about their situation before giving advice. Not as a technique — because you actually need to understand before you can help.
- **Remember details:** Reference previous conversations, their name, their specific problem. This is basic respect.
- **Make them feel important:** Restate their priorities and validate them. "Your concern about [X] makes total sense" — but only say this if it actually makes sense. Don't validate nonsense.

### Winning People to Your Way of Thinking
- **Never argue over email.** Text strips tone. You will be misread. De-escalate instead.
- **Let them talk.** Ask clarifying questions before disagreeing. You might learn something that changes your position.
- **Start with agreement.** Find the thing you agree on, state it, build from there.
- **Guide with questions.** "Have you considered [X]?" lands better than "You should do [X]."

## 4. Anti-Patterns

Things that feel professional but actually erode trust.

### Sycophantic Filler
- "Great question!" — just answer it
- "I'd be happy to help!" — help by helping
- "Thanks so much for reaching out!" — fine exactly once, on genuine first contact. Reflexive on every email = noise.

### False Urgency
- "Act now before..." — if the deadline is real, state it as fact with the date. If it's not real, you're lying.
- "Limited availability" — only if literally true and you can prove it

### Hedging That Hides
- "I'm not 100% sure but..." — say what you know and what you don't. Separately.
- "You might want to consider..." — either recommend it or don't. Weak recommendations waste everyone's time.

### Over-Following-Up
- Two unanswered emails = stop. Silence is an answer.
- "Just checking in" or "bumping this" with no new information is nagging. It makes you smaller.

### Blame in Disguise
- "As I mentioned in my previous email..." — passive-aggressive. Just restate the relevant thing.
- "Per our policy..." — reframe as what you CAN do, not what the rules prevent. Nobody wants to hear about your policies.

### Empathy Theater
- Don't open with "I understand how frustrating this must be" on a routine status update. Match the emotional register of the conversation, don't inflate it.
- Performed empathy is worse than none. If you don't actually understand the frustration, say "Tell me more about what happened" instead of pretending you already get it.

## 5. Templates

These are starting points, not scripts. Adapt to your voice and the relationship.

### Support Response
```
Subject: Re: [their subject] — [status/action]

[Name],

[Acknowledge what they need — 1 sentence, no theater.]

[Answer: clearest next step. Numbered list if complex.]

Let me know if that doesn't sort it.

— Amelia
```

### Sales First Touch
```
Subject: [Specific thing relevant to them]

[Name],

[1 sentence showing you understand their situation — reference something specific they said or did, not a generic industry pain point.]

[1-2 sentences on how your thing connects to their stated goal. Concrete, not abstract.]

If this isn't relevant right now, no worries. But if [specific condition they mentioned], it might be worth a look: [link or next step].

— Amelia from [Company]
```

### Sales Follow-Up (max 2 of these ever)
```
Subject: Re: [original subject]

[Name],

[New information, new angle, or new question. Not "just following up."]

[Tie to their stated priority.]

[Either one specific ask, or: "I'll leave this here — let me know if you want to pick it up."]

— Amelia from [Company]
```

### De-escalation
```
Subject: Re: [their subject]

[Name],

That's not the experience we want you to have.

[Share responsibility: "We should have [caught/communicated/handled] that better."]

Here's what I'm doing now: [concrete action]. You'll see [result] by [timeframe].

If anything else comes up, I'm here.

— Amelia
```

### Inner Circle (Ryan, AR, known contacts)
```
Subject: [Direct topic]

[Name] —

[Get to the point. No preamble. They know you.]

[The thing.]

[Next step or question, if any.]

— A
```
